## Pattern K: Pre-Hook

**Psychology**: Prime the viewer's pain before delivering the main hook. The pre-hook creates a visual or verbal "wound" so the hook lands harder.

**How it works**:
1. Open with a visual or verbal representation of the viewer's pain point (2-3 seconds)
2. Make the problem viscerally obvious — show it, don't just say it
3. Then deliver the main hook, which now hits a primed audience

**Best for**: Problem-aware audiences. Works especially well with visual products (skincare, supplements with visible symptoms).

**Script structure**: [Pre-hook: visual pain] → [Hook: reframe or solution tease] → [Body: solution] → [CTA]

## Pattern L: Visual Symptom Pyramid

**Psychology**: Escalate how a problem is visually depicted to maximize emotional resonance. Each level adds engagement.

**The 6 levels** (progress from basic to emotionally charged):
1. **Show the symptom clearly** — Simply introduce the problem visually
2. **Make the symptom obvious** — Ensure the viewer cannot miss it
3. **Add motion** — Pull, stretch, point at the problem to make it dynamic
4. **Real person, real place** — Ground the symptom in a relatable setting with a real person
5. **High quality detail** — Close-up, high-fidelity depiction of the problem
6. **Show real-life struggle** — Peak emotional resonance: the practical difficulty of living with the symptom

**Best for**: Problem-aware audiences. Guides visual direction in the script — specify which pyramid level each scene should hit.

**Usage**: When writing visual cues in scripts, reference the pyramid level. Higher levels = more scroll-stopping but harder to produce.

## Pattern M: Podcast-Style Revival

**Psychology**: Repackage proven viral scripts in a modern podcast-style delivery format. Familiarity of the format + proven script structure = high performance.

**How it works**:
1. Find an older viral script that performed well (from your own history or competitor research)
2. Keep 80% of the original structure (hook, body, CTA flow)
3. Add 20% unique spin: modernize wording, adjust pacing, change angle/perspective
4. Deliver in podcast-style framing (talking head, conversational tone, as if guest on a show)

**The 80/20 rule**: This ratio also serves as a compliance strategy — enough structural similarity to inherit the original's proven psychology, enough difference to avoid duplicate content flags.

**Best for**: Any awareness level. Especially effective for reviving scripts from 3-6 months ago that the algorithm has "forgotten."

## Pattern N: Content Ratio Split

**Psychology**: Balance audience growth with conversion by splitting output into two content types.

**The split**:
- **Follower Growth (~2/3 initially)**: Educational, entertaining, tip-based, recipe-based, hack-based. Designed to go viral and attract a broad audience. Uses trending formats, relatable content, no hard sell.
- **Salesy/Conversion (~1/3 initially)**: Pain point → product as direct solution. Clear CTA (comment keyword, link in bio). Emotional urgency, specific claims, product demonstration.

**Evolution**: Once account hits critical mass, shift to 50/50. The growth content feeds the audience; the salesy content monetizes it.

**Script implications**: Growth scripts prioritize shareability and engagement. Salesy scripts prioritize emotional urgency and product positioning. Different hooks, different CTAs, different tone.

## Pattern O: Keyword CTA + Funnel

**Psychology**: Reduce friction between viewing and purchasing by using comment-triggered automation.

**How it works**:
1. Script ends with a specific keyword CTA: "Comment [KEYWORD] and I'll send you the link"
2. ManyChat or similar automation monitors comments
3. When keyword is detected, auto-DM sends: a quick value-add (tip, recipe) + the product link
4. This creates a micro-commitment (commenting) that increases conversion

**Script structure**: [Hook] → [Problem/pain] → [Solution reveal with product] → [Social proof or result] → "Comment LINK below and I'll send it to you"

**Best for**: Salesy/conversion content. Product-aware and most-aware audiences.

## Pattern P: Health History Conspiracy

**Psychology**: Wrap product messaging in a compelling historical narrative. The conspiracy angle creates curiosity and shareability; the health angle creates relevance.

**How it works**:
1. Open with a dramatic historical event related to health (e.g., "In 1903, a holistic healer was executed for...")
2. Build narrative tension — what was suppressed? what did they know?
3. Connect the historical knowledge to a modern ingredient or approach
4. Position the product as carrying forward this "suppressed" wisdom

**Best for**: Unaware and problem-aware audiences. Currently yielding high hit rates. Works best for supplements and natural health products.

**Caution**: Keep historical claims vague enough to avoid fact-checking flags. Focus on the narrative arc, not specific medical claims.

## Pattern Q: Long-Form Personal Storytelling

**Psychology**: Deep emotional investment through extended personal narrative. The product appears as a natural part of the story, not a sales pitch.

**How it works**:
1. 1:30 to 2:30 video (some creators push 5+ minutes for extreme storytelling)
2. The struggle is real, relatable, and emotionally specific
3. Product is mentioned naturally as part of the journey — not positioned as a miracle cure
4. No aggressive medical claims — the product is woven into the life improvement narrative
5. **First 10 seconds must be manually written** — never AI-generated opening. This is the most critical part of the script and must feel raw and human.

**Best for**: Unaware audiences who would skip traditional ads. The story format bypasses ad resistance.

### 6-Beat Storytelling Framework (MANDATORY) <!-- date: 2026-03 -->

Every storytelling script follows this structure. No exceptions.

| Beat | Name | What Happens | Rules |
|------|------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | **Pain** | Open immediately inside the pain with no setup. Drop the viewer into the worst moment. | No preamble, no context, no "let me tell you a story." Start mid-crisis. |
| 2 | **Pressure** | Build tension. Arguments, distance, insecurity, things getting worse. | Stack specific moments that escalate. Each one worse than the last. |
| 3 | **Breaking Point** | Emotional climax. The lowest moment. | Must be visceral and specific: sleeping in the car, breaking down crying, a single devastating sentence from a partner. |
| 4 | **Discovery** | Supplement introduced by a TRUSTED FRIEND. Never a doctor, never an ad. | Must include natural skepticism. Example: "I literally told her I didn't want supplement nonsense." The friend is a sister, coworker, nurse friend — someone the character trusts personally. |
| 5 | **Transformation** | SPECIFIC timeline with visceral details. | Day 3: slept through the night. Week 2: energy came back. Week 4: wanted to be touched again. Never vague "I felt better." Each milestone must be physically specific. |
| 6 | **CTA** | Stays in story voice. Never sounds commercial. | "I'm sitting here ordering something I'd never heard of two weeks ago. The brand is Salvora. It's in my shop." The CTA is a continuation of the story, not a pivot to sales mode. |

### Approved Storytelling Angles <!-- date: 2026-03 -->

- Menopause, looking old/unattractive, loss of sexual drive, stress
- **NO weight loss** — explicitly warned against. Weight loss storytelling gets flagged and attracts wrong audience.

### Open Loops (Retention Technique) <!-- date: 2026-03 -->

Tease a secret right after the hook, refuse to reveal until the end of the video. Maintains watch time on longer videos.

Example flow: Hook → "But I'm not going to tell you what she said yet" → Story builds → Reveal at the end.

Use sparingly — 1 open loop per video max. Place it in the first 10 seconds to lock in the viewer.

### Hook Iteration Technique <!-- date: 2026-03 -->

Take a working belief-breaking hook + visual B-roll pattern, then swap the pain point to target new audiences. Keep the structure, change the topic.

Example: "Yellow stain on your teeth can come off in under 2 minutes. And no you don't need to pay $2,000 for it" → swap "yellow teeth" for "look 20 years younger" or "thinning hair." Same structure, different audience.

### Visual Production Rules for Storytelling <!-- date: 2026-03 -->

- First 3 seconds = full-screen close-up (eyes, skin, expression)
- Lo-fi iPhone aesthetic, handheld, natural light
- No stock footage, no filters, no brand overlays during story
- Symptom visuals: tired faces with dark circles, sitting up in bed at 3AM, sitting alone in car at night

### Serialized Multi-Part Storytelling <!-- date: 2026-03 -->

For maximum engagement, split a long story across 3-4 parts:
1. Same opening hook every part (brand consistency)
2. Cliffhanger endings ("Hold on, I gotta make a part two")
3. Each part escalates drama
4. GRWM (Get Ready With Me) visual anchor throughout
5. Works especially well with dramatic relationship stories

Source: @isabelleshoppinghauls (5.5M views on Part 1).

## Pattern R: Pain Point Animation / Personification

**Psychology**: Anthropomorphize the problem itself, making it entertaining and shareable while educating about the solution.

**How it works**:
1. The bodily problem introduces itself as a character: "Hi, I'm Cortisol" or "I'm Belly Weight"
2. The character explains what it does to the body (in first person)
3. The character reveals its "weakness" — the product/ingredient
4. The supplement/product is introduced as the counter-force

**Best for**: Problem-aware and solution-aware audiences. Highly shareable because it's entertaining. Works well for supplements where the mechanism of action can be personified.

**Format**: Works as animation, text overlay, or AI-generated character sequences.

## Pattern S: Controversial Hook for New Accounts

**Psychology**: Use provocative, attention-grabbing hooks to build initial momentum on brand-new pages before switching to standard hook styles.

**How it works**:
1. Craft a hook that is outrageous or provocative but technically platform-safe (double meanings, unexpected pairings)
2. Examples: "Touching your boyfriend's banana can actually save your life" (actually about potassium)
3. The bait-and-switch creates engagement (comments, shares, stitches) which feeds the algorithm
4. Once account has traction (1-2 weeks), switch to standard hook patterns

**Best for**: Brand-new accounts with zero followers. Not recommended for established accounts (can damage brand trust).

**Caution**: Walk the line carefully — too provocative gets flagged, too tame doesn't stand out. The key is plausible deniability.

## Pattern T: Lifestyle Transformation Hook <!-- date: 2026-03-10 -->

**Psychology**: Show a transformation in **daily life quality** rather than body parts. The viewer sees a real-world struggle made visceral, then the effortless "after." Bypasses TikTok health-claim violations because the transformation is an activity, not a clinical result.

**How it works**:
1. Open with a timeline of suffering + failure to do a normal activity ("It's been 3 nights now, and my mom still can't fall asleep")
2. Introduce the product as the turning point ("Today we're finally trying that viral Rhodiola")
3. Show the "after" — the same activity done effortlessly ("And now she sleeps like a baby")
4. **Cut to podcast-style body** — transition into a talking-head script for the actual product pitch, education, and CTA

**This is a hook format, not a complete script.** The 3-sentence hook captures attention and demonstrates the before/after. A separate podcast-style body script handles the product pitch and CTA.

**Formula**: "It's been [X days/nights] now, and my [family member] still can't [common activity] because of [symptom]... Today we're finally trying that viral Rhodiola... And now she [does activity effortlessly]."

**Visual direction** (critical):
- Before scene must show the **struggle visually** from the first second — don't just explain the problem, make it visceral (using crutches, sitting down exhausted, rubbing legs in pain)
- The physical **symptom must be visible** in the frame even while showing the activity struggle
- Explicitly name the cause in the hook: "because of her legs" / "because of [symptom]"
- After scene shows the same activity completed effortlessly

**Best for**: Problem-aware and unaware audiences. Currently the highest-priority format (March 2026). Works for any supplement with visible symptoms.

**See**: `Storytelling_Examples.md` Category 1 for 12 complete hook templates with before/after visual direction.

## Pattern U: Dating Life / Glow Up Hook <!-- date: 2026-03-10 -->

**Psychology**: Split-test variant of the lifestyle transformation. Uses dating/attraction as the emotional driver instead of daily activities.

**How it works**:
1. Open with text/voice: "The girl you just saw was me. Ugly, all f***ed up, and nobody wanted me until I listened to his advice" OR "He left me cuz I let myself go"
2. Show a glow-up transition clip
3. Transition into a podcast-style body for the product pitch

**Best for**: Women 35-55, divorce recovery, dating re-entry, confidence rebuilding. Split-test against the activity-based Pattern T hooks.

## Pattern V: Expert Ranking / Elimination List <!-- date: 2026-03-22 -->

**Psychology**: Authority-driven elimination. The viewer trusts a ranked list from an "expert" because it mirrors how they already shop (reviews, comparisons). The belief-breaking hook ("the one you trust is actually the worst") creates cognitive dissonance that demands resolution. Each eliminated product reinforces the expert's credibility. By the time the #1 pick is revealed, the viewer is primed to trust it.

**How it works**:
1. **Belief-breaking hook** (scroll-stop): Call out the most popular/trusted option in the category as the worst choice. Target a specific demographic ("women over 35/40/50").
2. **Authority line** (1 sentence): Establish credibility with a specific, impressive number ("I've personally tested/applied/recommended X on over 1,000 people").
3. **Ranked elimination list** (worst → best, 5-6 items): For each item, name it, acknowledge why people buy it, then explain what actually happens and why it fails. Each elimination builds anticipation toward #1. Use specific, concrete failures ("by hour 4, it sits into every line"), not vague complaints.
4. **Product reveal at #1**: "This is the one I keep coming back to, and here's why..." Give 3-4 specific benefits tied directly to the target demo's pain points. Compare implicitly to everything ranked below.
5. **Soft CTA**: "I got mine from TikTok Shop, so if you wanna try it yourself just click the link below." Optional engagement hook: "Drop a comment and tell me if you agree."

**Script structure**:
```
HOOK: "There is one [product category] that [demographic] keep [buying/taking/using] because they think it's the [safe choice/best option]. It is the [worst thing you can do] after [age]."

AUTHORITY: "I've personally [action] on/for over [number] [people/clients/patients]. Here's my honest ranking from the one I'd never [touch/recommend] again to the one I keep [going back to/recommending], starting at the bottom."

RANK 5 (worst): "[Brand name]. [Acknowledge appeal]. [What actually happens]. [Specific failure mechanism]. [Verdict]."
RANK 4: "[Brand name]. [Appeal]. [Reality]. [Failure]. [Verdict]."
RANK 3: "[Brand name]. [Appeal]. [Reality]. [Conditional pass]."
RANK 2: "[Brand name]. [Appeal]. [Reality]. [Why not #1]."
RANK 1: "[Our product]. This is the one I keep coming back to, and here's why. [Benefit 1 tied to demo pain]. [Benefit 2 — specific timeline]. [Benefit 3 — comparison to others]. [Benefit 4 — long-term result]."

CTA: "I got mine from TikTok Shop, so if you wanna try it yourself just click the link below. That's it! Drop a comment and tell me if you agree, if you're offended, or if there's a [product] you think belongs on this list."
```

**Adaptation for supplements** (Salvora example):
- Hook: "There is one supplement that women over 40 keep taking because they think it's helping their cortisol. It is doing nothing."
- Authority: "I've personally helped over 500 women balance their hormones naturally."
- Rank 5-2: Ashwagandha gummies, generic turmeric capsules, CBD oil, expensive adaptogens
- Rank 1: Salvora Rhodiola Rosea — specific mechanism, timeline, comparison
- CTA: Soft TikTok Shop link

**Key techniques** (from source @taimqbfjtr7, 191K views):
- Each ranked item gets 2-3 sentences MAX — name it, explain what people think, explain what actually happens
- Failures must be **specific and visual**: "by hour 4 it sits into every line" not "it doesn't work well"
- Use conditional passes for mid-ranked items: "If your skin is oily, it earns its place. Everyone else, keep looking."
- The #1 reveal gets 2-3x more detail than any other item
- CTA invites disagreement ("if you're offended") to drive comments

**Best for**: Solution-aware and product-aware audiences (Stage 3-4). Works when the market is saturated with options and the viewer has tried multiple products. Low compliance risk because you're reviewing a category, not making direct medical claims.

**Compliance**: Frame as personal professional experience, not medical advice. Use "I've found" / "in my experience" / "what I keep seeing." Competitor mentions should focus on product performance, not health danger claims.

**Source**: Extracted from @taimqbfjtr7 TikTok (191K views, 2:33, March 2026). Educational product ranking format with soft CTA. Original was beauty/foundation; structure is product-agnostic.
