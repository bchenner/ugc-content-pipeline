# Storytelling Confession

A structural variant of Talking Head where the face IS the B-roll. Zero camera movement, zero cutaways, zero production. All visual energy comes from emotional performance and caption timing. The imperfect, unstaged environment is the credibility signal.

## When to Use

- Raw emotional testimonials, confession-style stories, hero journey narratives
- When the STORY is the selling point and production would undermine authenticity
- Wife hero journey ("My husband kicked me out")
- Daughter hero journey ("My dad cheated on my mom") — can also use Split-Screen format
- Date story ("I went on my first date in 30 years")
- Younger man ("I went on a date with a woman 32 years older")
- Supplement saved my life ("I was about to give up")
- Burnout/loss of energy ("I was considering divorce")
- Time travel ("If I could go back 10 years")

## Visual Structure

```
[FULL SCREEN: Speaker in car/kitchen — static, single continuous take]
[TEXT: Sentence captions OR keyword karaoke — synced to emotional beats]
    ↓ emotional arc plays out entirely through facial performance
[FULL SCREEN: Same setup + product held up to camera in final beat]
```

- Speaker occupies 100% of the frame for the entire video
- ZERO B-roll. ZERO cutaways. ZERO cuts. Single continuous take
- The face replaces all production value. Every emotional beat (shock, tears, disbelief, laughter) is a visual event
- Product reveal always LAST (final 10-15 seconds). Story must reach emotional resolution first
- Text overlays are the only secondary visual element (added in post)

## Scene Layout

| Element | Frame Position | Size |
|---------|---------------|------|
| Speaker | Full screen, entire duration | 100% |
| Sentence captions | Lower third, white text | Overlay |
| Keyword karaoke | Mid-screen, yellow/white, large bold | Overlay |
| Subtitles | Bottom center | Overlay, added in post |
| Product (final beat only) | Speaker holds toward camera | Same frame |

## Camera

- Selfie-style, phone propped on dashboard (car) or on counter (kitchen)
- Eye-level or slight upward angle
- Close framing: chest to top of head (car), waist up (kitchen)
- 9:16 vertical
- NEVER move the camera. Phone is mounted/propped and never touched again
- Smartphone aesthetic, deep DOF, slightly off-center

## Settings

Two default environments, each signaling a different type of authenticity:

| Setting | Signal | Framing | Lighting |
|---------|--------|---------|----------|
| Car interior (nighttime) | "I had to get out of the house to tell you this" — spontaneous, urgent | Close (chest up), dashboard-propped | Dim ambient, parking lot light, dashboard glow |
| Car interior (daytime) | Same intimacy, slightly less dramatic | Close (chest up), dashboard-propped | Natural daylight through windshield |
| Kitchen counter | "I'm a real person in a real home" — domestic, relatable | Wider (waist up), counter-propped | Natural window light, overhead kitchen light |

The enclosed, unproduced setting is the format's credibility engine. Never stage or beautify it.

## Caption Styles

Two styles observed across reference videos:

### Sentence Captions
- Full phrases, white text, lower third position
- Synced to story beats, not individual words
- Used in: Wife Hero Journey, Wife Burnout
- Function: carries narrative, viewer reads along with the story

### Keyword Karaoke
- Individual words highlighted in yellow/white, staggered appearance
- Large, bold, mid-screen position
- Each word appearing is a micro-visual event
- Used in: Date Story / Younger Man
- Function: creates visual rhythm, forces attention, makes each word land individually

## Emotional Arc

The mandatory narrative structure. Every storytelling confession follows this arc:

```
Crisis → Empathy → Suspense → Resolution → Product/CTA
```

1. **Crisis**: Open with an extreme, emotionally loaded statement. Subject visibly distressed (teary, exhausted, raw)
2. **Empathy**: Draw the viewer into the story. Symptoms, relatable pain, "you know that feeling"
3. **Suspense**: Build tension. "Then she said something I didn't expect." Eyes wide, disbelief
4. **Resolution**: Emotional payoff. Tears of relief, hand over mouth, genuine surprise. The story resolves positively
5. **Product/CTA**: Only after emotional resolution. Subject holds product toward camera or names the brand. Calm, warm energy

## Beat Structure

This format has no visual transitions — the frame stays identical the entire video. The only visual change is the product appearing in the final beat.

```
CRISIS HOOK (0:00-0:06):
  Open inside the pain. No setup, no intro. "I'm sleeping in my car tonight."
  Visual: Static car/kitchen shot. No text yet.

SYMPTOM CASCADE (0:06-0:30):
  Stack relatable symptoms. Doctor dismissal. "I'm falling apart."
  Visual: Same shot. Sentence captions sync to key lines.

SUSPENSE PIVOT (0:30-0:45):
  "She said something I didn't expect." Sister/friend introduces the solution.
  Visual: Same shot. Caption timing creates micro-tension.

DISCOVERY + PROOF (0:45-1:20):
  Product name dropped casually. Third-party results: "By day five she slept through the night."
  Visual: Same shot. No product visible yet.

CTA (1:20-1:37):
  "The brand she used is called Salvora." Product held toward camera.
  Visual: ONLY visual change — product enters frame. Red arrow or shop button reference.
```

Key takeaway: ONE visual change in the entire video (product reveal at CTA). Everything else is the same static shot. The story carries the viewer, not production.

## Key Patterns

1. **The car as default setting.** Enclosed space, dim light, no staging = "unfiltered spontaneous confession." Kitchen serves the same function domestically
2. **ZERO camera movement** across all reference videos. Phone propped/mounted, never touched again
3. **Face IS the B-roll.** Every emotional beat (shock, tears, disbelief, laughter) happens on the face. Close framing ensures nothing distracts from microexpressions
4. **Product reveal always LAST** (final 10-15 seconds). Story must reach emotional resolution before any product mention
5. **Emotional arc is mandatory.** Crisis to Empathy to Suspense to Resolution to Product/CTA. Skipping steps breaks the format
6. **Setting signals authenticity.** Car = "I had to get out of the house to tell you this." Kitchen = "I'm a real person in a real home"
7. **Longer format works.** Reference videos range from 97s to 299s. The story carries the length, not production

## Key Difference from Standard Talking Head

| Aspect | Talking Head | Storytelling Confession |
|--------|-------------|----------------------|
| B-roll | Cutaway clips throughout | ZERO. None. By design |
| Cuts | Multiple (speaking to B-roll and back) | ZERO. Single continuous take |
| Camera movement | May shift angles between scenes | ZERO. Static, locked off |
| Production | Setting may be styled, lit intentionally | Unproduced. Car, kitchen, wherever they happen to be |
| Visual variety source | B-roll clips, angle changes | Emotional performance, caption timing |
| Primary trust signal | Speaker's face + production value | Speaker's raw emotion + unproduced environment |
| Typical length | 30-90s | 60-300s (story needs room) |

## Reference Image Groups

- **Base speaking image** (full prompt): Speaker in car interior at night (or kitchen counter with natural light), no product, emotional expression (teary/exhausted for crisis opener)
- **Product hold image** (modification mode): Same scene, speaker holds product toward camera, calm warm expression

No B-roll images. No end frame variants. One setting for the entire video.

## Scene Segmentation

- All scenes 8 seconds (Veo 3.1 constraint)
- 22-30 words per scene
- ONE angle for the entire video. No angle changes
- All scenes share a single reference image group (same setting, same framing)
- Emotional shifts happen through dialogue delivery and Veo's performance, not through visual changes
- B-roll density setting is irrelevant. This format has zero B-roll regardless of density mode
